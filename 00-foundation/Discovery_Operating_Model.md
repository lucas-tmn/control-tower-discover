# Discovery Operating Model

## Knowledge pipeline
Evidence → Knowledge → Trust → Canonical Knowledge → Decision Intelligence

Technology, including AI, is the consumer of this pipeline's output — not
the starting point.

## Discovery sources (three tracks, run in parallel — not sequentially)

| Track | Scope | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Existing Analytics** | 26 reports, semantic models, KPIs/measures, report logic, usage | In progress — Inventory Health & Forecast Accuracy validated first |
| **B — Business Discovery** | Interviews: Planners, Forecast team, Inventory team, Supply Planning, Buyers, Leadership | Not started — needs kickoff |
| **C — Additional Evidence** | SOPs, Excel files, email processes, shadow processes, manual workflows | Not started — can run alongside Track A once its report list is finalized |

Track A alone shows what the *reports* do. It cannot by itself confirm what
a *decision* is, who owns it, or what happens when the underlying data is
wrong — that requires Track B (the human side) and Track C (the
undocumented side). Running Track A in isolation risks Sprint 1 producing
evidence about the reporting estate rather than evidence about how Ashley
actually decides.

## Sprint 1 exit criteria
At least one worked decision example (see `Worked_Examples.md`) confirmed
from all three tracks simultaneously — the same decision, corroborated by
the report, an interview, and SOP/shadow-process evidence.

## Output
Decision Knowledge — discovered, then validated (Sprint 2–3), then made
canonical and reusable (Sprint 4).

## Future
Decision Registry → Decision Intelligence → Better Business Outcomes.
